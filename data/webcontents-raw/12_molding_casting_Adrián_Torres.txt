                            <p>As you know and you will have seen in my documentation, I like trains. üòÇ üöÇ So in this assignment I'm going to try to make a two-piece mold and create a little train. To do this I am inspired by two small models made of wood that I have in my room.</p>
                            
                            
                            <p><img src="images/week15/week15.03.jpg" width="60%"; max-width="700" /></p> 
                            
                            <p>To make the design of the mold I use <b>Rhinoceros.</b> I create the train, by extruding planes or using spheres. As it is going to be a mold in two parts, I cut the final shape of the train in half using a Boolean difference with a flat surface. Then I prepare the wax mold, that is, I empty a prism and add spheres that are the <b>registration</b> to join the two parts. It is through the wheels that I am going to introduce the resin; on both wheels I create a kind of funnel, one to fill and the other so that the air comes out.</p>

This is to improve the extraction of the final silicone and not have to use a knife or something to pry and damage the mold.</p>

                            <h1>3D Milling</h1>
                        
                            <p>At the Fab Lab Le√≥n we have two types of <b>mill ball end</b>, one of 3,175 mm and the other of 2 mm. The 2 mm one has the shortest cutting edge and also has a little conical shape. I'm going to use the 3,175mm bur because it has the longest shank, and I'm afraid it will collide the mandrel with the wax block, in addition to the conical shape of the mill.</p>
                        
                            <p><img src="images/week15/week15.06.jpg" width="50%"; max-width="700" /></p> 
                            
                            <p>I'm going to use <a href="https://github.com/fabfoundation/mods"><b>Mods CE üòé</b></a> to mill the mold, which is the program that I used to create the boards with the Roland MDX-20A that I explained in <a href="week04.html"><b>week 04</b></a>. Fran has been working on updating the interface. Although there is currently no program available for the Roland MDX-20A. So I test the simulation on the Monofab SRM-20. I put that I use a mill of 3,175 mm.</p>
                            
                            <span class="image main"><img src="images/week15/week15.05.jpg" alt="" /></span>
               
                            <p>Before using Mods CE in the Fab Lab Le√≥n, it was used <a href="http://fabmodules.org/"><b>Fab Modules.</b></a> It is an old version of Mods, but it works for the Modela and also gives you the time of the works.</p>
                        
                            <p>For this I open the terminal:
                            <ul><li> Run <b>fab</b>
                                <li> In the panel I choose the piece in this case the <b>.stl</b> of the train, the <b>Roland MDX-20</b> and <b>400 px</b> machine. 
                                <li> I open <b>Make_STL_RML.</b> At the top of the page there is a menu where I choose the <b>1/8 mill, wax, rough.</b>
                                <li> I verify the <b>diameter</b> of the tool, in this case it is 3.175 mm. The wax milling <b>speed</b> is 20 mm / s (if we wanted to mill polystyrene it would be 30 mm / s).
                                <li> I choose the <b>origin</b> X = 6 Y = 6.
                                <li> And finally we click on <b>Make PNG</b> -> <b>Make Path</b> -> <b>Make RML.</b> If the simulation is correct, click on <b>Send It!</b> ü§û</ul>
                            
                            <span class="image main"><img src="images/week15/week15.07.jpg" alt="" /></span>
                            
                        
                            <p>With this we get a <b>rough</b> first milling, we remove more chips faster. And remember, all the shaving you get, save it because you can create a new block of wax if you <b>melt it.</b></p>

                            <h1>Calculate the volume of the mold.</h1> 
                        
                            <p>Once we have milled the wax mold, we have to calculate the volume of silicone that we are going to need. To do this by reviewing documentation from other students, there are two options. Use <b>rice</b> or use <b>water.</b></p>

                            <p>If we use rice, following the documentation by <a href="http://fabacademy.org/2019/labs/leon/students/alejandro-ausejo/week10.html"><b>Alejandro Ulecia,</b></a> which is based on filling the molds with rice, emptying this rice in a plastic cup and marking this volume and then filling it. I mark the plastic cup and weigh its contents, it is 66 grams with the plastic cup (the cup weighs 6 grams).</p>
                        
                            <span class="image main"><img src="images/week15/week15.13.jpg" alt="" /></span>
                        
                            <p>But this system doesn't convince me much because the rice grains do not enter all the holes in the wax mold, so I discard using it.</p>
                        
                            <p>The other way is to use water, in this case I use <a href="https://fabacademy.org/archives/2013/students/nunez.pablo/weeks/week7.html"><b>Pablo Nu√±ez</b></a> documentation. In which the mold is filled with water and that amount of water is weighed. In theory, the density of water and weight are the same. In my case it is 80 grams with the plastic cup, which is equivalent to 80 milliliters.</p>
                        
                            <span class="image main"><img src="images/week15/week15.14.jpg" alt="" /></span>
                        
                            <p>Once I have calculated the volume of the mold, <b>protecting</b> the work area and myself, I go to work with the silicones. The apron has the <a href="https://textile-academy.org/"><b>Fabricademy </b></a> logo. üòç </p>

                            <h1>Silicone mold.</h1>
                            
                            <h2>VytaFlex¬Æ 40</h2>
                            
                            <p>As I have explained in the Group Assignment part and what the manufacturer tells us, this silicone should be mixed: <b>1 Part A: 1 Part B</b> by weight or volume. So after measuring the volume of the mold and giving me 80 grams with the cup (5 grams), I decided to round and put 40 grams of Part A and 40 grams of Part B. This is the result, it is always difficult to adjust liquids with different viscosities. üòÖ</p>
                            
                            <span class="image main"><img src="images/week15/week15.16.jpg" alt="" /></span>
                        
                            <p>Before mixing the two parts, spray the wax mold with the <a href="https://www.smooth-on.com/products/universal-mold-release/"><b>Smooth On Universal Mold Release.</b></a> A small layer is sufficient.</p>
                        
                            <p><img src="images/week15/week15.17.jpg" width="60%"; max-width="700" /></p>
                        
                            <p>Once the release agent is done, I mix the two parts very carefully not to cause bubbles, for about 2 minutes. Then I pour it slowly with the help of the mixing stick. The next thing I do is tap around so that the bubbles are removed and rise upwards. I leave the mold like this for 16 hours.</p>
                        
                            <span class="image main"><img src="images/week15/week15.18.jpg" alt="" /></span>
                        
                            <p>After more than 16 hours had passed, I removed the silicone from the wax mold. Thanks to the edge that you leave around, it is removed very well. There are no bubbles left, and the fit with the registrations is fine. I will need to use elastic bands to hold the two parts and the openings to pour the resin and let the air out will be enough.</p>
                        
                            <span class="image main"><img src="images/week15/week15.19.jpg" alt="" /></span>

This time I adjusted the quantities a lot, I should have added more, I hope the silicone comes out of the mold. üò¨ The next thing I do is tap around so that the bubbles are removed and rise upwards. I leave the mold like this for 24 hours.</p>

                            <h1>Castings</h1>
                        
                            <h2>Smooth-Cast¬Æ 300</h2>
                        
                            <p>The first casting that I am going to do is with VytaFlex¬Æ 40 silicone and low viscosity polyurethane resin. As I explained in the Group Assignment, this resin is very fast to cure and creates an exothermic reaction, so you have to be very careful.</p>
                            <p>As the manufacturer explains, this resin has a pot life of 3 minutes and a cure of 10 minutes. The mix ratio is: <b>1 Part A: 1 Part B</b> by volume. The fact is that now I do not have to use the measure of 80 grams (this was to create the silicone). Since I want to leave a sample of the material and there are more silicones that I can fill, I make a mixture of 40 grams of each part, because the volume of this resin is similar. <b>Important:</b> As Neil explains in class, if we have leftover material from both parts, we must mix it and make the reaction, if we throw it without mixing it can be dangerous.</p>
                        
                            <span class="image main"><img src="images/week15/week15.22.jpg" alt="" /></span>
                        
                            <p>Before mixing the two parts, I prepare the silicone, joining the two parts with <b>elastic bands.</b></p>
                        
                            <p><img src="images/week15/week15.23.jpg" width="60%"; max-width="700" /></p> 
                        
                            <p>I mix the two parts in less than 2 minutes, and slowly pour it into the silicone. And the bubbles begin to rise outwards. What happens is that this resin is quite old and has not been well closed at some point and the reaction is not the same, more material comes out. Once 10 minutes have passed, I remove the silicone and the train has gone well. ü§ó</p>
                        
                            <span class="image main"><img src="images/week15/week15.24.jpg" alt="" /></span>
                        
                            <p>The train will need post-processing to remove the excess material.</p>
                        
                            <p>At the Fab Lab Le√≥n we also have dyes to dye the resin pieces, it is called <a href="https://www.smooth-on.com/products/so-strong/"><b>SO-Strong‚Ñ¢</b></a> and it is from the Smooth-on brand. And Pablo had blue fluorescent powders, it glows in the dark. I make two other 10 gram mixes of each part A and B again; one I dye blue and the other I put the fluorescent powders.</p>
                        
                            
                            <span class="image main"><img src="images/week15/week15.25.jpg" alt="" /></span>
                        
                            <p>Once I have the three trains, the white, the blue and the one with the powders that glow in the dark, I start to remove the excess material with files and sandpaper.</p>
                        
                            <p><img src="images/week15/week15.26.jpg" width="60%"; max-width="700" /></p>
                            
                            <p>And this is the result of the three trains. I love the one that <b>glows in the dark,</b> it's incredible. üòç</p>
                        
                            <span class="image main"><img src="images/week15/week15.27.jpg" alt="" /></span>
                        
                        
                            <h2>Smooth-Cast¬Æ ONYX‚Ñ¢ FAST</h2>
                            
                            <p>Thanks to <a href="http://fabacademy.org/archives/2015/eu/students/diosdado_barroso.raul/index.html"><b>Raul Diosdado</b></a> (student of the Fab Academy in 2015), I can use this resin, because he sent it to us from Extremadura. Thank you very much Raul. ü§ó</p>
                        
                            <p>This resin is similar to Smooth-Cast¬Æ 300, although much faster, it has a pot life of 2.5 minutes and cures in 10 minutes. The pieces are black. The mix ratio is: <b>1 Part A: 1 Part B</b> by volume. In this case, by weighing you can see that the volume is not equal to the weight, part A is more liquid and needs more quantity.</p>
                        
                            <span class="image main"><img src="images/week15/week15.32.jpg" alt="" /></span>
                        
                            <p>I mix the two parts in less than 1 minute, and slowly pour it into the silicone. The material that I have left is a sample for the Group Assignment. Like the other Smooth-Cast¬Æ 300 resin, it creates an exothermic reaction, so be careful. After 10 minutes, remove the silicone mold carefully, if we leave it much more we can damage the silicone because this resin is very hard. The result is perfect, you have to remove the excess material.</p>
                        
                            <span class="image main"><img src="images/week15/week15.33.jpg" alt="" /></span>
                        
                            <p>After filing and sanding the little train (I have to say it is a super hard material üòÖ), it is very cool in black.</p>
                            
                            <p><img src="images/week15/week15.34.jpg" width="60%"; max-width="700" /></p>
                            
                        
                            <h2>Candle wax</h2>
                        
                            <p>At the Fab Lab Le√≥n we had scented candles, so I thought, I can make a <b>candle</b> in the shape of my little train. So I put to heat water in the pot and inside I put a container where the original candle will melt. In Spain this process is called <b>"Ba√±o Maria".</b> <b>Important:</b> Be careful with hot water and melted wax. üî•üî•</p>
                        
                            <p><img src="images/week15/week15.28.jpg" width="60%"; max-width="700" /></p>
                        
                            <p>Since I have no wick, I reuse the one that came in the candle, and put it inside the silicone. I close the silicone with elastic bands and let the wick protrude from the chimney of the train.</p>
                        
                            <span class="image main"><img src="images/week15/week15.29.jpg" alt="" /></span>
                        
                            <p>Once the liquid wax has been poured, I let it cool, and so that it finishes cooling completely, I put it in the <b>refrigerator.</b> This is the little candle on the little train. It is sad to light it hehe.</p>
                            
                            <p><img src="images/week15/week15.30.jpg" width="70%"; max-width="700" /></p>
                        
                            <h2>Chocolate</h2>
                        
                            <p>The coolest moment of this week has arrived. üòã Create a <b>chocolate</b> in the shape of the little train. üç´ üöÇ</p>
                            <p>The first thing to do is <b>wash</b> the food silicone with soap and water. Then, we will butter the mold so that the chocolate does not stick and we can not release it. While we bring water to a boil and in another saucepan, add the chocolate (just like the candle, to the "Ba√±o Maria").</p>
                        
                            <span class="image main"><img src="images/week15/week15.37.jpg" alt="" /></span>
                        
                            <p>Then we will put it in the freezer for a couple of hours (the first test I did in the fridge and it does not work, the chocolate is still soft). And we already have our little chocolate train.üòã Be careful with the fireplace, which breaks easily.</p>
                        
                            <p><img src="images/week15/week15.43.jpg" width="70%"; max-width="700" /></p>
                            
                        
                            <h2>PLASTICRETE (STONE-LIKE FINISH)</h2>
                            
                            <p>The next resin I use is acrylic, which when it dries its finish imitates the stone of the sculptures. They are two components, one powder and the other in liquid. Percentage of mixture in weight: 100 powder + 60 liquid. I put 26 grams of powder and 18 grams of liquid.</p>
                        
                            <span class="image main"><img src="images/week15/week15.35.jpg" alt="" /></span>
                        
                            <p>I mix both components and pour it little by little into the silicone. After a curing of more than 8 hours, I notice that enough liquid has come out of the silicone, as if the piece had "sweat". I open the mold and see that the material has shrunk and that material is missing, that it is outside the mold. ü§Ø</p>
                        
                            <span class="image main"><img src="images/week15/week15.36.jpg" alt="" /></span>
                            
                        
                            <p>And this is the result. Material was missing and the chimney was broken. Reading further the manufacturer's instructions, he comments that the proportion of the <b>liquid</b> can be varied to <b>45 parts</b> and obtain a more viscous resin where the time of use decreases, the drying time of the product accelerates, the piece will be less porous and valid for the outside. When the lock-down is over, I will try it again with more time.</p>

                            <p>Surely in the not too distant future, I will apply this manufacturing process to the possibility of creating several model pieces, much faster and in greater detail than 3D printing.</p>
                        
                            <p>This is the progress and result of everything I have applied this week. I am very proud and have had a lot of fun. üòä</p>
